GENDER BASED VIOLENCE

Gender based violence (GBV) and abuse describes a range of oppressive acts which cover a spectrum of violence and abuse committed primarily but not exclusively against women. GBV continues to exist due to social inequality between men and women. Both financial and societal vulnerability created by inequality andans societal conditioning around accepted behaviour mean that women are more likely to experience both GBV and sustained abuse. There are many forms of abuse which affect women and girls because of their gender including, but are not limited to:

  • Domestic abuse - financial dependency, threat or use of physical or sexual violence or psychological or emotional manipulation
  • Sexual violence, abuse and exploitation
  • Female genital mutilation
  • Forced marriage
  • ‘Honour’ based violence
  • Stalking
  • Trafficking

People may experience one or multiple forms of oppression, and can happen in person, over the phone or online. Certain groups are more vulnerable to this kind of behaviour and can face additional barriers to getting help. These include but are not exclusive to migrant women, LGBTQI groups, black women and ethnically minoritised people and those with physical or mental disability. Domestic abuse and violence can also affect men and due to stigma as well as fear are less likely to seek help – you are not alone and help is available.

HOW TO KNOW IF YOU ARE EXPERIENCING GBV

The most difficult step is coming to terms with the fact that you or someone you know is experiencing abuse. Although every situation is unique, if you do not feel comfortable or something just doesn’t feel right, it could be that the other person is behaving abusively. This list from Women’s Aid identifies common behaviours for those experiencing GBV:

  • Verbal abuse: shouting; mocking; accusing; name calling; swearing; verbally threatening.

  • Pressure tactics: sulking; threatening to withhold money or taking away possessions or freedoms unless they comply, taking the children away; threatening to report you to services ; threatening or attempting self-harm and suicide; withholding or pressuring you to use drugs or other substances.

  • Disrespect and constant criticism: persistently putting you down or undermining you, either on your own or in front of other people; not listening or responding when you talk; interrupting when you speak.

  • Breaking trust: lying to you; withholding information from you; being jealous; having affairs, breaking promises and shared agreements.

  • Isolation: preventing you from seeing friends and relatives; keeping you inside the house.

  • Harassment: following you; checking up on you; not allowing you any privacy (for example, opening your mail, going through your laptop, tablet or mobile), repeatedly checking to see who has phoned you; embarrassing you in public; accompanying you everywhere you go.

  • Physical Threats: making angry gestures; using physical size to intimidate; shouting you down; destroying your possessions; breaking things; punching walls; wielding a weapon of any kind; threatening to kill or harm you and/or the children; threatening to kill or harm family pets; threats of suicide.

  • Sexual violence: using force, threats or intimidation to make you perform sexual acts; having sex with you when you don’t want it; forcing you to look at pornographic material; constant pressure and harassment into having sex when you don’t want to, forcing you to have sex with other people; any degrading treatment related to your sexuality or to whether you are lesbian, bisexual or heterosexual.

  • Physical violence: punching; slapping; hitting; biting; pinching; kicking; pulling hair out; pushing; shoving; burning; strangling, pinning you down, holding you by the neck, restraining you.

  • Denial: saying the abuse doesn’t happen; saying you caused the abuse; saying you wind them up; saying they can’t control their his anger; being publicly gentle and patient; crying and begging for forgiveness; saying it will never happen again.

If you are still unsure Women’s Aid have an online questionnaire which may help make things clearer for you.

HOW TO GET HELP

The routine enquiry has been set up in all maternity, sexual health, health visiting, substance use and mental health settings to ensure that all pregnant people are asked if they are experiencing abuse. This query should always be made when you are alone to make sure you are safe and able to talk and ideally the first time they see you. All professionals are trained to identify behaviours indicating they could be experiencing domestic violence or abuse and may enquire outside of these areas if they have concerns. However if you are experiencing GBV or abuse please don’t wait to be asked but seek help. There are many organisations set up to help to help in a sensitive and safe way to protect you and anyone in your household who is affected.
